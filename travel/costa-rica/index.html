A TRIP I ACTUALLY PLANNED · 10 DAYS · 12 GUESTS · COSTA RICA

We skipped the $30K wedding and booked a jungle instead.

Ten days, twelve people, two coasts, and a wedding day that cost us exactly $0 in venue fees. Snorkeling with nurse sharks, a surprise mariachi band, hot springs under a volcano, and vows on a beach beneath trees older than any building we toured back home. THE SPARK

A bus quote broke us: $2,000 to shuttle 25 people, twice.

We did everything right. A dedicated wedding email account. Twenty different venues toured across Colorado. Line items priced down to the napkins. And somewhere in that process we noticed a pattern: the second the word “wedding” touched anything — the flowers, the shuttle, the cake — the price tripled. The breaking point was a quote for a 25-person shuttle running two short routes: $2,000. We already throw parties for the people we love more often, and for a lot less, than $30K buys for one night.

So we asked ourselves what actually mattered. Not “does this have the right lighting,” but three real things: getting to travel and chase adventure while we're young enough to enjoy it, staying financially free enough to keep doing that, and having a wedding day that felt romantic instead of like a fire drill. We had friends who'd been begging for an excuse to take a group trip abroad. So instead of booking a ballroom, we booked ten days in Costa Rica — a real vacation with the people we love most, with a wedding stitched into the middle of it.

HOW IT UNFOLDED

Five days of adventure before we said “I do.”

DAY 1

Chaos, Customs, and Costa Rican Driving

Airport by 4am, because we never pay extra for the luxury of sleeping in. We landed, met the whole family at a tiny airport café, and within fifteen minutes had already absorbed the first rule of Pura Vida: nobody is in a hurry, including the rental car agent who wanted to chat for half an hour before handing over the keys. Then came the real initiation — driving through San José. No lane discipline, no consistent signage, motorcycles treated as more of a suggestion than a vehicle class, and mountain highways that decide mid-curve whether they're one lane or two. Everyone arrived at the first stop a little rattled and a lot more bonded.

DAYS 1–2

A Private Jungle Lodge

We had the whole property to ourselves — steak dinners, the best margaritas anyone in the group had ever had, and a long table where twelve people finally exhaled together. Mornings meant walking into rainforest so dense it felt like a different planet: leaves the size of torsos, vines tangled six directions at once, colors too vivid to be real. (One overnight scare involving a mysterious object in a cabin wall turned out to be an electrical outlet, not a hidden camera — but for a few hours it was the best group drama of the trip.)

DAY 3

Arrival on the Caribbean Coast

The drive south delivered exactly what hours of research had promised: black sand giving way to turquoise water, a laid-back downtown, and a boutique jungle resort south of Puerto Viejo that had the whole group audibly gasping at check-in. While rooms got ready, half the group scouted Playa Chiquita in the rain (worth it) and the other half found tacos. Dinner was Argentinian food at a candlelit spot tucked just off the main road — the kind of place you'd never find without someone doing the homework first.

DAY 4

Snorkeling with Nurse Sharks and a Guide Who Sees Everything

8am sharp on the beach in Cahuita, snorkel gear on, and two hours covering serious ground across the reef inside Cahuita National Park — widely considered the best snorkeling in the country. Stingrays swam directly beneath us, and when someone spotted “a really big fish” that turned out to be a nurse shark, our guide swam straight at it just to show us there was nothing to fear. The afternoon hike through the park topped it: a guide who spotted bats camouflaged against tree bark that not one of us would have noticed in a hundred years of looking — sloths, monkeys, iguanas, and tree frogs included. That night, the group threw together a full birthday celebration from scratch for the youngest guest.

DAY 5

Dolphins, a Private Beach, and Finding “The Spot”

Second boat tour: Manzanillo National Wildlife Refuge, water striped in a dozen shades of blue depending on what the ocean floor was doing beneath it. We circled Monkey Point, found a starfish, and then a multi-species pod of wild dolphins surfaced right next to the boat and stayed with us. From there, a walk to a stretch of beach so remote and untouched we had it entirely to ourselves for over an hour. Later that afternoon, on a whim, we turned down an unmarked road toward Punta Uva — and knew within thirty seconds we'd found the ceremony site. Officiant, photographer, and décor were locked in by dinner.

DAY 6 · THE WEDDING

A beach ceremony under trees older than any building we toured.

The morning started slow — bridal makeup at 10:30, a first look in the backyard of our rented villa while family watched from the patio and someone put music on. By 1pm the photographer had us at Manzanillo National Park for portraits (sweating through formalwear in tropical heat is a real occupational hazard, and there's photographic proof), then on to the ceremony site at Punta Uva: an aisle lined with centuries-old trees, wild monkeys swinging through the canopy overhead, and a full setup — arch, chairs, coconut water for guests, a champagne toast station — built entirely from scratch for a few hours on a beach we didn't own and didn't pay a single venue fee for.

The vows were the real thing — a little shaky, a lot of tears held back, no rehearsal-perfect delivery, just two people meaning every word. From there: SOCA, a restaurant we'd chase down again in a heartbeat — a fountain, walls of plants, string lighting, and a menu that made even the appetizer round feel like an event. Dinner was interrupted by a full mariachi band that appeared with zero warning and played for the better part of an hour while we had our first dance. Toasts ran long, the guest sombrero got signed as a keepsake, and dessert showed up as a last-minute tres leches cake nobody ordered and everybody needed. We ended the night in the villa pool. Ten days of planning, and the one day that had to go right, went exactly right.

DAYS 7–10

The trip kept going after “I do.”

DAY 7

Baby Sloths and Happy Tears

Breakfast the morning after turned into happy-tears-in-public territory — the kind of gratitude that sneaks up on you once the adrenaline wears off. A private tour of the Jaguar Rescue Center delivered toucans, agoutis, and a room of three sloth babies so small they didn't look real. Dinner that night was a beach club with the whole crew, still riding the high.

DAYS 8–9

Waterfalls, a Volcano, and a Troop of Coatis

An early beach walk in the rain, coffee at a rainbow-colored café, and a drive inland to La Fortuna — where Los Lagos Resort delivered on its promise of dozens of natural hot springs. A flat tire rerouted one morning from the Hanging Bridges to La Fortuna Waterfall instead — Maui-green and worth every minute of the detour. A two-mile uphill hike to the Arenal viewpoint rewarded patience: the volcano's peak cleared of cloud cover the moment the hike was finished. Lake Arenal National Park delivered a full troop of coatis begging for snacks on their hind legs, plus a water slide daring enough to convert even the skeptics.

DAY 10

The Long Way Home

One final scenic mountain drive back to San José, a last group meal, and flights out — closing a ten-day trip that started as a rebellion against a $30K quote and turned into the best version of our wedding we could have possibly imagined.
